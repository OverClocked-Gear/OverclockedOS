# Capital One Infrastructure Live Lab Pilot

## Draft Status

Instructor-facing working draft for a graduate synchronous workshop. This does not create graded quiz, exam, or assessment-secure material.

## Course Placement

- Course: Digital Strategy
- Best-fit session: Week 2 / Module 2 - Digital Evolution & Infrastructure
- Session rationale: The syllabus objective asks students to assess an organization's digital infrastructure for readiness for digital growth and innovation. The scenario adds AI-enabled guidance as a responsible-launch decision.
- Course mode: Graduate, synchronous
- Workshop length: 35-45 minutes
- Common context: Capital One / banking_finance_insurance

## Scenario

Capital One is evaluating an AI-enabled financial guidance capability within its digital experience. Leadership must determine what infrastructure, identity, governance, data, compliance, and customer-experience conditions must be in place before a pilot can responsibly launch. Teams should not assume that a specific Capital One capability currently exists. Treat this as an executive decision scenario grounded in Overclocked infrastructure, AI readiness, and the processed banking-finance cards.

## Pre-Class Preparation

- Review Overclocked Chapter 4 concepts: infrastructure as competitive capability, TOE Framework, and Five-Layer Technology Stack.
- Skim the processed banking_finance_insurance source/profile/signal cards.
- Bring one example of a financial-services digital interaction where trust, friction, identity, or data quality affects the customer experience.
- Expected preparation time: 20-25 minutes.

## Workshop Timing

- 0-5 minutes: Scenario framing and decision question.
- 5-12 minutes: Team infrastructure readiness scan.
- 12-22 minutes: Governance, risk, and customer-experience conditions.
- 22-32 minutes: 90-day recommendation build.
- 32-40 minutes: Executive readouts.
- 40-45 minutes: Instructor debrief and synthesis.

## Team Roles

- Customer Experience Lead: defines the customer job, trust threshold, and friction risks.
- Infrastructure Lead: maps identity, data, security, APIs, analytics, resilience, and integration conditions.
- Governance Lead: owns compliance, privacy, explainability, accountability, and escalation conditions.
- Strategy Lead: turns the analysis into a 90-day executive recommendation and scale-up KPI.

## Facilitation Sequence

1. Apply TOE: identify the technology, organization, and environment conditions that shape the launch decision.
2. Apply the Integrated Infrastructure Stack: name which layers must be ready before an AI guidance pilot can be exposed to customers.
3. Apply the Insight Value Chain: decide what data can be captured, integrated, analyzed, synthesized, acted on, and measured responsibly.
4. Apply Kinetic Strategy: separate speed from recklessness; define where velocity is useful and where adaptability or governance must slow the launch.
5. Apply Digital Horizon Planning: classify the first pilot as Horizon 1 optimization, Horizon 2 extension, or Horizon 3 transformation.

## Decision Deliverable

Each team prepares a 90-day recommendation with five parts:

1. What must be true before launch: identity, data quality, consent, compliance review, escalation paths, model-risk controls, service recovery, and measurement ownership.
2. What should be piloted first: a narrow, low-risk financial guidance use case where advice can be bounded, explained, and escalated to human support.
3. What should not yet be attempted: autonomous financial decisions, high-stakes personalized recommendations without review, opaque model outputs, or broad launch across all customer segments.
4. Primary risk: customer trust damage from inaccurate, unclear, noncompliant, or poorly escalated guidance.
5. KPI or experiment governing scale-up: a paired measure such as guidance completion with safe-escalation rate, customer confidence with complaint rate, or successful task resolution with compliance exception rate.

## Instructor Debrief Questions

- Where did teams choose speed, and where did they choose restraint?
- Which layer of the infrastructure stack became the launch constraint?
- Did the team treat compliance as a blocker, or as a design condition?
- What evidence would leadership need before moving from pilot to scale?
- How did the recommendation change when viewed through the customer trust lens?

## Strong-Recommendation Indicators

- Makes a clear launch/no-launch distinction for the next 90 days.
- Defines preconditions rather than jumping to technology selection.
- Connects identity, data, governance, infrastructure, and customer experience.
- Names what not to attempt yet.
- Uses a scale-up KPI that balances customer value and enterprise risk.
- Shows executive tradeoff logic, not just feature enthusiasm.

## Common Reasoning Failures

- Treating AI as a feature instead of an operating-system decision.
- Ignoring identity, consent, explainability, or escalation.
- Assuming more personalization is always better.
- Measuring adoption without measuring trust, complaints, exceptions, or service recovery.
- Repeating the undergraduate website inspection instead of making an executive launch decision.
- Making unsupported current claims about Capital One capabilities.

## Optional One-Page Post-Class Executive Memo

Students may convert the workshop output into a one-page executive memo with:

- Recommendation: pilot, defer, or narrow the scope.
- Conditions before launch.
- Pilot use case and excluded use cases.
- Primary risk and mitigation.
- Scale-up KPI.
- One unresolved decision for leadership.
