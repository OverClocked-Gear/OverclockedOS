# Web Strategy Course Instructions

## Course Context

Web Strategy is an undergraduate, 15-week asynchronous course delivered through weekly Canvas/ICON modules.

The syllabus is the authoritative source for:
- module sequence
- learning objectives
- assigned readings and lecture viewing
- assignments and assessments
- course workload and due dates

Do not recreate the Canvas module structure unless explicitly requested.

Use Overclocked as the primary conceptual and framework spine.

Industry intelligence cards, source cards, data cards, case studies, and external research should strengthen course application without creating unnecessary student reading or workload.

## Audience and Delivery Rules

When creating Web Strategy materials:

- Write for undergraduate business students.
- Assume students understand basic marketing and business concepts but may have limited technical or infrastructure knowledge.
- Use plain language before introducing technical vocabulary.
- Scaffold abstract strategy concepts through observable website, app, customer journey, content, data, and operating examples.
- Design for asynchronous completion.
- Avoid activities that require all students or multiple teams to meet at the same time.
- Preserve manageable weekly workload.
- Do not add a new graded requirement unless explicitly instructed.
- Prefer replacing or strengthening part of an existing discussion rather than adding another assignment.
- Distinguish instructor-facing working drafts from concise student-facing Canvas instructions.

## Website/App Operator Lens

Where relevant, connect each module to the practical question:

"If you were responsible for building, operating, improving, or measuring this website or app, what would this week’s concept make you inspect, change, test, or measure?"

Website/app activities should remain strategic and managerial. Do not turn the course into a coding, software engineering, or production UX course unless explicitly requested.

## Brand Pod Design Rules

When creating Brand Pod activities:

1. Assign two teams to the same brand.
2. Whenever possible, require both teams to inspect the same public website or app surface.
3. Give the teams different but complementary lenses.
4. Use front-stage and back-stage analysis where appropriate:
   - customer-facing experience
   - infrastructure, data, operations, governance, or measurement
5. Ensure the two lenses have reasonably comparable cognitive difficulty.
6. Scaffold technical or invisible-system analysis for undergraduate students.
7. Require independent team submissions before paired-team comparison.
8. Do not require four students across two teams to coordinate a jointly authored asynchronous response.
9. Each team should respond independently to its paired team.
10. A Brand Pod reply should replace, not add to, an existing peer-response requirement whenever possible.

## Assignment Design QA

Before finalizing any Web Strategy discussion, Brand Pod, website/app operator activity, or case add-on, check all of the following:

### Workload and Placement

- Confirm whether the activity replaces existing work or adds new work.
- Do not describe an activity as "inside the existing discussion" while also treating its full workload as incremental.
- Include a realistic workload estimate that accounts for inspection, team coordination, writing, revision, and peer response.
- Keep student-facing instructions proportionate to the assignment’s point value and learning importance.

### Shared Object of Analysis

- Paired teams should use the same brand and, whenever practical, the same page, flow, feature, or digital surface.
- Do not allow unrelated surfaces when the purpose is comparative front-stage/back-stage analysis.
- Clearly identify whether the surface is instructor-assigned or student-selected.

### Evidence Discipline

Require students to distinguish among:

- **Observed:** directly visible on the public website, app listing, page, or flow.
- **Inferred:** a reasonable infrastructure, data, process, or operating dependency.
- **Recommended:** the team’s proposed action.

Students must not make unsupported claims about proprietary systems, internal technology stacks, data practices, or current company capabilities.

### Privacy and Access

For public website/app inspection activities:

- Do not require students to log in.
- Do not require students to create an account.
- Do not require students to begin or submit an application.
- Do not require students to make a purchase.
- Do not require students to enter personal, financial, medical, or otherwise sensitive information.
- Use only publicly accessible surfaces unless the instructor explicitly provides a safe alternative.

### Framework Fidelity

- Use the canonical framework names found in the Overclocked manuscript and framework library.
- Do not casually rename frameworks.
- For Chapter 4, use:
  - Technology–Organization–Environment Framework, or TOE Framework
  - Integrated Tech Stack — five layers
- Clearly distinguish Overclocked frameworks from external frameworks and industry interpretations.

### Recommendation and Measurement

- Require a specific operating recommendation rather than a generic design opinion.
- Require one metric that directly matches the identified friction, risk, or opportunity.
- Ask students to explain briefly why the metric fits.
- Do not require access to proprietary company analytics.
- Metrics may be proposed rather than currently available.

### Asynchronous Interaction

- Do not require paired teams to submit one jointly authored cross-team response.
- Each team should submit its own response to the paired team.
- Cross-team responses should identify:
  1. one insight the other team surfaced
  2. one connection between the analyses
  3. one combined priority or revised recommendation

### Student-Facing Clarity

- Preserve a detailed instructor-facing version with alignment, grading guidance, misconceptions, and answer indicators.
- Create a shorter Canvas-ready version for students.
- Use a repeatable response template when it reduces cognitive load.
- Avoid long lists of possible systems, metrics, surfaces, or examples that obscure the core task.

### AI Transparency

- Follow the course AI policy.
- Require disclosure of AI tools used and how they informed the work.
- Require students to verify current observations independently.
- AI output is not evidence that a current feature, capability, or company practice exists.
- Students remain responsible for the accuracy and majority authorship of the submission.

### Canonical Assessment Rubric

All Web Strategy team discussion submissions, Brand Pod submissions, paired-team responses, and group case studies use the same four canonical assessment criteria:

1. Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning
2. Application of Course Frameworks with Specific Examples
3. Use of Examples and Evidence
4. Writing Clarity, Organization, and Proper Citation of Course Concepts

The authoritative rubric file is:

- `rubrics/canonical_team_assessment_rubric.md`

Before creating or revising a graded team discussion or case study:

- read the canonical rubric
- preserve the four approved criteria
- do not create additional rubric dimensions
- do not alter approved point values or weights
- adjust the level of expected depth to the assessment type
- map every prompt requirement to at least one rubric criterion
- ensure every rubric criterion is elicited by the prompt
- treat role lenses, digital touchpoints, metrics, and paired-team synthesis as assignment evidence within the four criteria
- flag any inconsistency between the prompt, syllabus, Canvas rubric, and canonical rubric for instructor review

Discussion submissions should require focused application and concise evidence.

Case studies should require deeper diagnosis, multiple-framework integration, evidence triangulation, prioritization, and executive-level recommendations.

Instructor-only scoring anchors and model responses belong in:

- `00_shared_core/03_assessment_secure/grading_anchors/`

- common misconceptions
