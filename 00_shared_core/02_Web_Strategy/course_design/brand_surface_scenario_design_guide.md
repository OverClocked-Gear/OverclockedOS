# Brand, Surface, and Scenario Design Guide

## Purpose

This guide helps the instructor design Web Strategy discussions, Brand Pods, website/app operator activities, and case-study applications that connect Overclocked frameworks to real digital work.

The core design principle:

The brand is not the assignment. The surface, customer job, operating scenario, role pairing, evidence boundary, recommendation, and metric are the assignment.

A recognizable brand can increase student engagement, but the brand should be selected only when its digital surfaces support the module objective, Overclocked framework, operating role lenses, and expected evidence.

## Design Sequence

Use this sequence before drafting any Brand Pod or applied discussion:

1. Identify the module objective.
2. Select the primary Overclocked framework.
3. Choose the industry and brand.
4. Select the exact digital surface or instructor-provided exhibit.
5. Define the customer or user job.
6. Define the operating scenario and decision.
7. Assign complementary operating-role lenses.
8. Define what students can directly observe.
9. Define what students may reasonably infer.
10. Define what students must not claim.
11. Require one recommendation and one matched metric.
12. Check privacy, access, stability, and fallback requirements.
13. Map the task to the four canonical rubric criteria.
14. Compress the final student-facing version for Canvas.

## Framework Contract Rule

A framework contract is the short assignment-level agreement that tells students exactly how Overclocked frameworks should be used. It prevents students from guessing whether the task requires a specific framework, a role-specific framework emphasis, or a bounded framework choice.

Each graded discussion, Brand Pod, website/app operator activity, and case-study prompt should identify:

- Required framework: the primary Overclocked framework every team must apply.
- Role-specific framework emphasis: how each assigned team or role lens should use the required framework or a supporting framework.
- Optional extension framework: one or two additional frameworks students may use if they strengthen the diagnosis, recommendation, or measurement logic.
- Frameworks to avoid or not force: frameworks that may be related to the broader course but would distract from the module objective, surface, scenario, or workload.

Framework use is evidence under the canonical rubric, especially Application of Course Frameworks with Specific Examples. It may also support Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning, Use of Examples and Evidence, and Writing Clarity, Organization, and Proper Citation of Course Concepts when students use the model to structure the argument.

Common student misuses include:

- name-dropping a framework without applying it
- defining the framework instead of using it to analyze the surface or scenario
- using too many frameworks for a short discussion
- selecting a poor-fit framework because it sounds familiar
- treating a role lens, brand, or digital surface as a substitute for framework application
- forcing a framework that does not improve the diagnosis, recommendation, or metric

If students are expected to choose a framework, provide a bounded set of appropriate choices and explain what a good choice should accomplish.

## Brand Selection Principles

A brand should be selected because it supports the learning objective, not merely because it is famous.

A good brand choice should have:

- a public digital presence students can inspect safely
- enough depth to support the assigned framework
- a clear customer, buyer, user, or stakeholder job
- a meaningful operating decision
- enough visible evidence to support analysis
- enough complexity to support complementary role lenses
- relevance to the semester's industry and role-coverage goals
- manageable workload for asynchronous undergraduate students

Avoid brands when:

- the experience requires login, purchase, private data, account creation, or application submission
- the public surface is too thin to support analysis
- the assignment would require proprietary analytics or internal knowledge
- the brand is selected only because students recognize it
- the company context overwhelms the module objective
- the surface is likely to change or disappear before submission
- the surface is heavily personalized in ways that create unfairly different student experiences

## Digital Surface Selection

The digital surface is the shared object of analysis.

Possible surfaces include:

- homepage
- landing page
- product page
- product-comparison page
- search-results page
- category page
- checkout entry or cart flow, if no purchase is required
- app-store listing
- onboarding entry point
- public help center
- support path
- FAQ or policy page
- public account/sign-in entry page
- loyalty page
- content hub
- email signup flow
- pricing page
- demo request page
- B2B customer portal overview
- marketplace listing or seller/buyer page
- public AI/chatbot entry point, if no private data is required

### Surface Quality Test

A surface is strong when students can answer:

- What is the user trying to do?
- What decision, friction, or trust issue is visible?
- What role or team would own improvement?
- What systems, content, data, or workflows likely support it?
- What can be observed directly?
- What must be treated as inference?
- What metric would indicate improvement?
- How does this surface connect to the module's framework?

A surface is weak when:

- it is mostly decorative
- it has no clear customer job
- it does not support both Brand Pod teams
- it requires private access
- it cannot support a recommendation
- it encourages visual preference rather than strategic diagnosis
- it requires students to speculate about proprietary systems

## Surface Types

### Live Public Surface

Use when the current digital experience matters.

Benefits:

- real-world relevance
- current UX evidence
- easy student access

Risks:

- pages change
- personalization may vary
- access may break
- students may overclaim current capabilities

Use only after instructor verification.

### Instructor-Captured Surface

Use when consistency and fairness matter.

Examples:

- dated screenshot
- PDF capture
- short screen recording
- annotated page or flow
- archived public page

Benefits:

- all students analyze the same evidence
- supports fair comparison
- preserves the assignment if the live page changes

### Instructor-Created Scenario Exhibit

Use when the learning objective requires data or operating context students cannot observe publicly.

Examples:

- mock funnel analytics
- conversion table
- product backlog
- campaign performance summary
- CRM segment brief
- merchandising plan
- customer-journey map
- incident or service-recovery scenario
- measurement dashboard
- AI feature proposal
- B2B buyer workflow

Scenario exhibits are especially useful for Analytics, CRM, Performance Marketing, Platform/Technology, and Digital Strategy / General Manager lenses.

## Customer or User Job

Every activity should identify the job the user is trying to complete.

Examples:

- compare products
- evaluate trust
- sign up
- begin a quote
- learn how a service works
- solve a support problem
- find a product
- assess pricing
- understand delivery or availability
- choose a plan
- complete onboarding
- evaluate a case study
- request a demo
- return to a service
- manage a relationship

The job should be specific enough to support a recommendation and metric.

Weak:

"Use the website."

Stronger:

"Evaluate whether the public product-comparison page helps a new customer choose the right option without increasing trust or compliance concerns."

## Scenario Design

A scenario should place students inside an operating decision.

Strong scenarios identify:

- decision-maker or operating team
- business/customer problem
- constraint or tradeoff
- time horizon
- assigned roles
- available evidence
- expected recommendation
- matched metric

Useful scenario types include:

| Scenario Type | Typical Decision |
|---|---|
| Friction reduction | Which journey step should be improved first? |
| Trust building | How should uncertainty be reduced without adding too much friction? |
| Acquisition efficiency | How should paid traffic and landing-page experience work together? |
| Merchandising | What should be shown, grouped, promoted, or explained? |
| Lifecycle | How should an anonymous visitor become a known or retained customer? |
| Measurement | What should be instrumented and which KPI should govern improvement? |
| Business-model evolution | What digital model should the company test or scale? |
| Platform/ecosystem | Which participant, integration, or network effect matters most? |
| Service recovery | How should the digital experience respond when a promise fails? |
| AI readiness | What should be automated, augmented, governed, or deferred? |
| Innovation backlog | What should enter the next sprint or experiment? |
| B2B enablement | What self-service, documentation, quote, reorder, or integration capability matters? |

Avoid scenarios that only ask students to "review the website" or "find improvements."

## 360° Role-Lens Design

Use the canonical operating roles from course_design/digital_operating_roles.md:

- Digital Product & Experience
- Performance Marketing
- Digital Merchandising & Content
- CRM, Lifecycle & Loyalty
- Analytics & Experimentation
- Platform, Technology & Operations
- Digital Strategy / General Manager

Do not require every role in every module.

Use no more than two or three primary roles in a normal weekly application.

Assign roles that fit the module objective and surface.

Examples:

| Module Focus | Strong Role Pairings |
|---|---|
| Infrastructure | Product + CRM/Lifecycle vs. Platform/Technology + Analytics |
| Digital Consumer | Product + CRM/Lifecycle vs. Merchandising/Content + Analytics |
| Content Strategy | Merchandising/Content + CRM/Lifecycle vs. Product + Analytics |
| Paid Media | Performance Marketing + Analytics vs. Product + Merchandising/Content |
| GTM | Product + Performance Marketing vs. CRM/Lifecycle + Analytics |
| Measurement | Analytics + Performance Marketing vs. Product + CRM/Lifecycle |
| B2B Platforms | Product + Platform/Technology vs. CRM/Sales Enablement + Analytics |
| AI/Future | Product + Platform/Technology vs. Analytics + Strategy/GM |

Use Digital Strategy / General Manager as the synthesis lens when students must reconcile tradeoffs across roles.

## Brand Pod Design Principles

Brand Pods work best when:

- two teams are assigned to the same brand
- both teams inspect the same public surface or instructor-provided exhibit
- each team has a different but complementary role lens
- teams submit independently before comparing
- the paired response replaces the normal peer response
- the paired response synthesizes rather than praises
- both teams produce one recommendation and one matched metric
- the assignment remains within the existing discussion workload

Do not require four students across two teams to coordinate one jointly authored asynchronous response.

Each team should independently respond to its paired team.

A strong paired response identifies:

1. one insight the other team surfaced
2. one connection between the two analyses
3. one combined priority from the Digital Strategy / General Manager perspective

## Evidence Discipline

Every applied activity should distinguish:

- **Observed:** directly visible on the assigned public surface, instructor-provided exhibit, or assigned course material
- **Inferred:** a reasonable operating, infrastructure, data, role, process, or measurement dependency
- **Recommended:** the team's proposed action

Students must not present inference as fact.

Students must not claim to know proprietary systems, vendors, algorithms, databases, internal performance, customer data, analytics, security controls, or internal decision processes unless provided in assigned course material.

AI output is not evidence that a current feature, capability, metric, or company practice exists.

## Recommendation and Metric Discipline

Every applied activity should require:

- one specific operating recommendation
- one metric that matches the problem and recommendation
- one sentence explaining why the metric fits

The metric may be proposed. It does not need to be publicly available.

Metric examples:

| Role Lens | Possible Metric Families |
|---|---|
| Product | task completion, activation, feature adoption, abandonment, time-to-task |
| Performance Marketing | CAC, ROAS, click-to-conversion, landing-page conversion, incremental lift |
| Merchandising/Content | product-page conversion, search success, add-to-cart, AOV, content engagement |
| CRM/Lifecycle | opt-in, repeat purchase, retention, lifecycle progression, churn, engagement |
| Analytics/Experimentation | instrumentation coverage, test lift, data quality, time-to-insight, attribution confidence |
| Platform/Technology | uptime, latency, error rate, recovery time, release quality, incident rate |
| Strategy/GM | customer outcome, business impact, risk reduction, resource priority, scale-readiness |

Avoid vague metrics such as "engagement" or "customer satisfaction" unless students define how the metric would be captured and why it fits.

## Privacy, Access, and Safety Rules

For public digital-surface activities:

- do not require students to log in
- do not require account creation
- do not require personal, financial, medical, or sensitive information
- do not require purchase
- do not require students to begin or submit an application
- do not require contacting customer support
- do not require testing security controls
- do not require students to download apps
- do not require students to bypass access restrictions
- do not require students to share personal device information

For regulated industries such as finance, healthcare, insurance, education, or government services, review access and privacy rules especially carefully.

## Stability and Fallback Rules

Before publishing a live-surface activity, the instructor should:

- verify the link immediately before release
- save a dated screenshot or PDF as a fallback when practical
- confirm that the page is publicly accessible
- confirm that the page is appropriate for all assigned roles
- confirm that the page does not require personal data
- record the verification date
- provide instructions if the page changes

Use a fallback artifact when:

- the surface is likely to change
- the assignment depends on a specific layout or flow
- fairness requires all students to inspect the same evidence
- the brand personalizes the experience heavily
- the surface is seasonal or campaign-based

## Canvas Compression Rule

Instructor-facing drafts may include:

- alignment tables
- grading concerns
- strong-answer indicators
- weak-answer indicators
- misconceptions
- role rationale
- setup checklists
- verification flags
- workload analysis

Student-facing Canvas prompts should be shorter and should include only what students need to complete the task:

- purpose
- assigned surface
- role lens
- access rules
- evidence labels
- task instructions
- submission format
- paired response
- four canonical rubric criteria
- AI transparency

Do not expose instructor-only industry intelligence, grading anchors, model answers, or secure assessment content in Canvas.

## Rubric Alignment

Use only the four canonical criteria:

1. Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning
2. Application of Course Frameworks with Specific Examples
3. Use of Examples and Evidence
4. Writing Clarity, Organization, and Proper Citation of Course Concepts

Role analysis, surface observations, metrics, recommendations, and paired synthesis are evidence within those four criteria.

Do not create new rubric categories unless the instructor explicitly approves.

## AI Transparency

When AI use is allowed, the activity should require students to disclose:

- tool used
- purpose of use
- prompt or concise prompt summary when required
- what the team checked, corrected, rejected, or rewrote

AI may help brainstorm, organize, or critique. AI may not replace student observation or verify current company capabilities.

## Instructor Preflight Checklist

Before publishing, confirm:

- module objective identified
- primary framework selected
- brand selected for instructional fit
- exact surface or exhibit selected
- surface verified
- fallback artifact saved if needed
- customer/user job defined
- operating scenario defined
- role pairings assigned
- role difficulty balanced
- evidence boundaries clear
- privacy/access rules satisfied
- recommendation and metric required
- workload fits the existing assignment
- canonical rubric alignment complete
- Canvas version compressed
- current claims verified or removed
- AI guidance included
- no proprietary or raw industry intelligence exposed

## Common Design Mistakes

Avoid:

- selecting a brand before defining the learning objective
- using a generic homepage when a task-specific surface would be stronger
- allowing paired teams to inspect unrelated surfaces
- asking undergraduates to infer too much from too little visible evidence
- turning the activity into visual design critique
- forcing all roles into one weekly activity
- using roles as rubric categories
- requiring private account access
- requiring cross-team synchronous coordination
- creating hidden workload
- using live pages without fallback plans
- publishing unverified current claims

## Good Example Pattern

A strong Brand Pod design looks like this:

Module objective:
Understand how digital infrastructure enables trust, reliability, and customer experience.

Framework:
Technology–Organization–Environment Framework and Integrated Tech Stack.

Brand:
Capital One.

Surface:
Instructor-assigned public product, help, security, sign-in entry, or application-entry surface.

Scenario:
Determine how to improve customer trust or task completion without weakening security, privacy, compliance, or operational reliability.

Team A:
Digital Product & Experience + CRM, Lifecycle & Loyalty.

Team B:
Platform, Technology & Operations + Analytics & Experimentation.

Synthesis:
Digital Strategy / General Manager combines customer-facing and infrastructure recommendations into one operating priority.
