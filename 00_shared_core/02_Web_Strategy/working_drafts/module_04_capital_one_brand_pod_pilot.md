## Student-Facing Prompt

Use Capital One as the common brand context. Inspect one current public Capital One website or app surface that you can access without using a private account. Do not claim that a specific feature exists unless you can observe it directly or connect it to course materials. Your team response should focus on infrastructure, not just visual design.

### Team A Lens: Customer-Facing Experience

Write 150-250 words. Identify one public Capital One website or app surface, such as a homepage, product page, help/support path, application path, sign-in entry point, or mobile-app store listing. Explain what a customer is trying to do and which infrastructure layers must work together for that experience to feel trustworthy and usable. Consider onboarding, navigation, trust cues, authentication entry points, support, account-management expectations, disclosure clarity, or friction. Identify one friction point, risk, or missing connection you observe or reasonably infer from the experience. Recommend one operating improvement that would reduce friction without weakening trust, security, or compliance. Select one metric that would indicate improvement, such as completion rate, help-search success, form abandonment, authenticated task success, support deflection quality, or customer confidence signal.

### Team B Lens: Behind-The-Scenes Infrastructure

Write 150-250 words. Use the same brand context, but analyze what must happen behind the scenes for the surface to work responsibly. Identify one public Capital One website or app surface and map the infrastructure layers that likely need to coordinate: identity, data integration, security, compliance, analytics, APIs, content systems, operational resilience, or customer support workflows. Do not speculate about proprietary systems. Instead, describe the categories of infrastructure that must be reliable for a regulated financial-services experience. Identify one friction point, risk, or missing connection that could occur if those layers are not integrated. Recommend one operating improvement that would strengthen reliability, trust, or decision quality. Select one metric that would indicate improvement, such as authentication success, incident rate, data-quality issue rate, disclosure completion, response latency, task recovery, or escalation accuracy.

### Paired-Team Response

After both teams post, write 75-100 words together identifying one insight the other team surfaced that your team missed, one place where the two analyses connect, and one combined priority you would recommend to Capital One. The strongest paired responses should connect customer friction to infrastructure choices rather than treating them as separate problems.

## Instructor Note

Keep this as an application layer inside the existing Module 4 discussion. The activity uses Capital One as a shared inspection object because Overclocked Chapter 4 uses Capital One as an infrastructure case, while the processed banking cards add the regulated-services lens: trust, risk, data governance, compliance, and service reliability. Students may inspect current public surfaces, but the course draft should not assert current Capital One capabilities beyond what students observe.

## Strong-Answer Indicators

- Identifies a specific public surface and a specific customer job.
- Connects visible experience to multiple infrastructure layers.
- Uses trust, risk, data, compliance, or resilience as operating constraints.
- Recommends an improvement that preserves security and compliance.
- Selects a metric that directly matches the friction or risk.
- Distinguishes observed evidence from reasonable inference.

## Weak-Answer Indicators

- Describes visual design only.
- Makes unsupported claims about Capital One internal systems.
- Treats security or compliance as obstacles rather than design constraints.
- Recommends generic improvements without a metric.
- Ignores the paired-team connection between customer experience and infrastructure.
