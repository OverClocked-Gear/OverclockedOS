# Module 4 Discussion: Capital One — Infrastructure, Trust, and Banking’s Next Horizon

## Draft Status

Instructor-facing working draft. This is a streamlined revision of the existing Module 4 Capital One infrastructure activity for undergraduate business students. It replaces the two-team Brand Pod structure with a single two-person team discussion using the original Team A customer-facing lens.

This is not a new assignment, not a full Brand Pod, and not a paired-team activity. It should replace the existing Module 4 discussion work rather than add another requirement.

## Course Placement

- Course: Web Strategy
- Module: Module 4 — Enabling Infrastructure
- Audience: undergraduate business students
- Delivery: asynchronous
- Assessment type: existing team discussion
- Team structure: assigned partner/team of two, per syllabus
- Syllabus timing: Week 5, Module 4; Online Discussion Submission due Feb. 19

## Module Objective Alignment

Module 4 syllabus objectives:

- Understand the Role of Digital Infrastructure
- Differentiating Between Internal and External Infrastructure
- Recognize the Impact of Infrastructure Decisions

The revised assignment supports these objectives by asking students to examine one public Capital One digital surface and reason backward from a visible trust or customer-job issue to the infrastructure capabilities that likely matter behind the scenes. The assignment keeps the focus on customer trust, identity continuity, platform readiness, and infrastructure capability rather than on technical architecture details students cannot observe.

## Instructional Rationale

The purpose is to help undergraduates understand infrastructure as business capability. Students do not need to know cloud engineering, cybersecurity architecture, blockchain design, or bank systems to reason responsibly about infrastructure. They need to ask:

- What customer job or trust issue is visible?
- What systems, data, identity, compliance, or operating dependencies probably support that experience?
- Which capabilities matter now, which should be extended next, and which future capabilities should be monitored but not rushed?

The required LLM Support Step helps students translate technical concepts into plain language, test their assumptions, and avoid overclaiming. The LLM is a learning assistant, not a source of evidence.

## Framework Contract

### Required Frameworks

1. Technology–Organization–Environment Framework, or TOE Framework
2. Integrated Tech Stack — five layers
3. Digital Horizon Planning / Three Horizons

### Framework Use Expectations

Students should use:

- TOE Framework to explain how a banking infrastructure decision is shaped by technology capability, organizational readiness, and external/regulatory/customer expectations.
- Integrated Tech Stack — five layers to connect the public surface to likely capability layers: Digital Utilities, Source of Truth, Connective Tissue, The Brain, and The Handshake.
- Digital Horizon Planning / Three Horizons to separate near-term operating improvement from future capability monitoring.

### Required Horizon Language

Students should classify their analysis using:

- Horizon 1: optimize today’s digital banking experience
- Horizon 2: extend platform readiness
- Horizon 3: future capability to monitor but not rush

### Frameworks Not To Force

Do not force:

- vendor-stack identification
- cybersecurity testing
- full cloud architecture analysis
- full blockchain architecture analysis
- crypto investment analysis
- internal Capital One system claims
- AI/future-state claims beyond the scenario framing

## Role Lens

Use a single-team role lens:

- Digital Product & Experience
- CRM, Lifecycle & Loyalty
- Digital Strategy / General Manager

Students should think like the team responsible for connecting a public customer experience to trust, identity continuity, relationship quality, operating tradeoffs, and strategic sequencing.

Platform/Technology & Operations and Analytics & Experimentation remain supporting dependencies students may infer. They are not assigned role lenses in the student task.

## Scenario Framing

Capital One operates in a regulated banking environment where digital surfaces must balance usefulness, trust, identity, privacy, compliance, service continuity, and platform readiness.

Students may use crypto, stablecoins, tokenized deposits, tokenized assets, and programmable finance only as scenario drivers. These topics should be treated as future infrastructure pressures that raise questions about trust, ownership, settlement, identity, compliance, programmability, and customer confidence.

The assignment must not claim that Capital One currently offers crypto, stablecoin, tokenized deposit, tokenized asset, wallet, custody, blockchain, AI, or tokenization products or capabilities. Students should not discuss whether crypto or tokenized assets are good investments.

## Assigned Surface Protocol

The instructor should assign or approve one public Capital One surface. Appropriate options include:

- public product-information page
- public help or security information page
- public sign-in entry page
- public application-entry page before personal information is requested
- public support or trust-related page

Students must state the surface examined and access date.

Students must not log in, create accounts, begin applications, submit applications, enter personal or financial information, contact support, test security controls, download apps, or use private account information.

## LLM Support Step

Students must use an LLM as a technology translator and hypothesis generator. The LLM may help them understand terms, map possible dependencies, and review their draft for overclaiming. It may not replace public-surface observation, assigned course concepts, or cited evidence.

Suggested prompts:

**Prompt 1: Explain the technology in plain language.**

> I am an undergraduate business student studying digital infrastructure in banking. Explain [tokenized deposits / stablecoins / programmable finance / identity continuity / platform readiness] in plain language. Focus on trust, ownership, settlement, identity, compliance, and customer experience. Do not make claims about Capital One’s current products or capabilities.

**Prompt 2: Map infrastructure capabilities to TOE, Integrated Tech Stack, and Three Horizons.**

> Based on this public surface observation: [paste brief observation], help me brainstorm possible infrastructure capabilities using the TOE Framework, the Integrated Tech Stack five layers, and Three Horizons. Label each idea as a hypothesis, not a fact.

**Prompt 3: Review the draft for unsupported claims or overreach.**

> Review this draft for unsupported claims, overreach, or statements that sound like we know Capital One’s internal systems or current crypto/tokenization capabilities. Identify what should be changed to Observed, Inferred, or Recommended.

Required disclosure:

- AI tool used
- prompt or concise prompt summary
- what the LLM helped the team understand
- what the team verified, corrected, rejected, or rewrote

## Student-Facing Prompt Draft

Capital One is a useful setting for studying infrastructure because banking customers expect digital experiences to be convenient, trustworthy, secure, reliable, and connected across relationship moments. For this discussion, your team will examine one public Capital One digital surface and use course frameworks plus LLM-supported sensemaking to connect what customers see to the infrastructure capabilities that likely matter behind the scenes.

In approximately 300-400 words, submit one response from your two-person team. There is no paired-team reply.

Use the required structure:

1. Surface examined and access date
2. Customer job or trust issue
3. Observed
4. Inferred
5. Horizon analysis:
   - Horizon 1: optimize today’s digital banking experience
   - Horizon 2: extend platform readiness
   - Horizon 3: future capability to monitor but not rush
6. Recommendation: one Horizon 1 or Horizon 2 operating improvement
7. Metric and why it fits
8. Sources and AI disclosure

Your recommendation should improve customer trust, identity continuity, platform readiness, or infrastructure-enabled service quality. Your metric should match the problem and recommendation. The metric may be proposed; it does not need to be publicly available.

## Evidence Discipline

Students must distinguish:

- Observed: directly visible on the public surface or assigned course material
- Inferred: a reasonable infrastructure, data, identity, process, compliance, measurement, or operating dependency
- Recommended: the team’s proposed Horizon 1 or Horizon 2 operating improvement

Students must not claim to know proprietary Capital One systems, vendors, databases, algorithms, security controls, analytics, compliance operations, internal metrics, AI capabilities, blockchain capabilities, crypto offerings, or tokenization capabilities.

AI output is not evidence that a current Capital One feature, product, system, or capability exists.

## Canonical Rubric Alignment

Use the four authoritative criteria only:

1. Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning
2. Application of Course Frameworks with Specific Examples
3. Use of Examples and Evidence
4. Writing Clarity, Organization, and Proper Citation of Course Concepts

Do not add role performance, LLM use, website inspection, metric selection, or horizon classification as new rubric categories. Treat those requirements as evidence within the four canonical criteria.

| Assignment requirement | Applicable rubric criterion | Evidence expected | Common grading concern |
|---|---|---|---|
| Identify surface, customer job, or trust issue | Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning | Specific customer job or trust issue tied to the public surface | Describing the page generally without diagnosing a customer or operating issue |
| Apply TOE Framework | Application of Course Frameworks with Specific Examples | Technology, organization, and environment factors connected to the banking infrastructure decision | Listing TOE terms without using them to explain the decision |
| Apply Integrated Tech Stack — five layers | Application of Course Frameworks with Specific Examples | Two or three relevant layers connected to the visible experience | Speculating about specific proprietary systems or vendors |
| Apply Digital Horizon Planning / Three Horizons | Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning; Application of Course Frameworks with Specific Examples | Clear separation of Horizon 1 optimization, Horizon 2 platform readiness, and Horizon 3 future monitoring | Treating speculative future capabilities as immediate recommendations |
| Distinguish Observed, Inferred, and Recommended | Use of Examples and Evidence | Public-surface observations separated from reasonable inference and proposed action | Treating LLM output or internal-system assumptions as evidence |
| Recommend one Horizon 1 or Horizon 2 operating improvement | Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning | Feasible improvement tied to trust, identity continuity, platform readiness, or service quality | Recommending a broad future technology launch without readiness logic |
| Propose one matched metric and explain fit | Use of Examples and Evidence; Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning | Metric tied directly to the problem and recommendation | Using vague metrics such as engagement without explaining fit |

## Strong-Answer Indicators

- identifies a specific customer job or trust issue
- explains infrastructure as business capability, not technical trivia
- applies TOE, Integrated Tech Stack, and Three Horizons to the same issue
- uses the LLM to clarify and challenge assumptions rather than generate unsupported facts
- separates observed evidence from reasonable inference
- treats tokenization and programmable finance as future infrastructure pressures, not investment topics or current Capital One claims
- recommends one realistic Horizon 1 or Horizon 2 operating improvement
- proposes a metric that matches the recommendation
- discloses AI/source use transparently

## Weak-Answer Indicators

- keeps the old Team A/Team B Brand Pod structure
- claims Capital One currently offers crypto, tokenized assets, wallets, custody, stablecoins, or blockchain products without assigned evidence
- treats crypto as investment advice
- describes technical architecture without connecting it to customer trust or operating capability
- speculates about internal systems, vendors, security controls, or analytics
- uses the LLM as evidence
- lists frameworks without applying them
- recommends a Horizon 3 future capability as if it should be rushed immediately
- proposes a metric unrelated to the customer job or trust issue

## Common Student Misconceptions

- Infrastructure means only servers or cloud.
- Business students need to know exact technical architecture to discuss infrastructure.
- Tokenization is mainly an investment topic.
- More personalization or automation is always better in banking.
- Compliance is only a barrier rather than a design condition.
- A proposed metric must already be public.
- AI can verify current company capabilities.
