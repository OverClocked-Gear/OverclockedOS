# Midterm Review PowerPoint Outline: Connecting the Dots Across Chapters 1-5

Purpose: Create an instructor-facing outline for a short review video that helps students prepare for the midterm without giving them a model answer.

Note: The review video should not provide a model answer. It should teach students how to read the scenario, select frameworks, prioritize causal drivers, and build a recommendation.

## Slide 1: What the Midterm Is Testing

- Purpose: Set expectations for synthesis.
- Key teaching points:
  - The midterm tests strategic reasoning across Chapters 1-5.
  - Strong answers connect concepts rather than recite them.
  - Students should diagnose, apply frameworks, recommend, and conclude.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Flow from scenario to diagnosis to frameworks to recommendation.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Emphasize synthesis over breadth.
- Connection to midterm: Establishes the main performance expectation.

## Slide 2: How to Read a Scenario

- Purpose: Show students how to extract useful evidence.
- Key teaching points:
  - Read for symptoms, root causes, constraints, tradeoffs, and choices.
  - Underline facts that point to leadership, strategy, disruption, infrastructure, and business model issues.
  - Do not import unsupported outside facts.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Annotated scenario excerpt with labels for symptoms and constraints.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Remind students that scenario facts are evidence.
- Connection to midterm: Helps students use the scenario rather than summarize it.

## Slide 3: Identifying the Central Problem or Opportunity

- Purpose: Teach prioritization.
- Key teaching points:
  - A strong diagnosis identifies the central issue, not every issue.
  - Symptoms may include churn, rising acquisition costs, slowing engagement, or weak alignment.
  - Root causes may involve adaptability, velocity, leadership readiness, adoption friction, infrastructure limits, or business model pressure.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Symptom-to-root-cause funnel.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Warn against treating all facts as equally important.
- Connection to midterm: Supports the first essay section.

## Slide 4: Kinetic Strategy as the Synthesis Framework

- Purpose: Review the required framework.
- Key teaching points:
  - Kinetic Strategy evaluates adaptability and velocity.
  - Adaptability asks whether the firm can reconfigure strategy, resources, business model, and organization.
  - Velocity asks whether the firm can learn, decide, experiment, and execute quickly enough.
  - The answer may be low adaptability, low velocity, both, or a mismatch.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Adaptability x Velocity matrix.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Students should use Kinetic Strategy to organize the diagnosis.
- Connection to midterm: Required framework.

## Slide 5: Choosing Supporting Frameworks Wisely

- Purpose: Prevent framework laundry lists.
- Key teaching points:
  - Students should choose two supporting frameworks that best explain the case.
  - Framework choice should follow the diagnosis.
  - Strong framework use explains root causes, tradeoffs, or strategic options.
  - Weak framework use lists definitions without applying them.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Decision tree for choosing frameworks.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Explicit warning against framework laundry lists.
- Connection to midterm: Supports bounded framework selection.

## Slide 6: Chapter 1 Support — Leadership and Change Readiness

- Purpose: Show when Chapter 1 is useful.
- Key teaching points:
  - Use leadership/change readiness when alignment, culture, decision rights, or change capacity is central.
  - Relevant concepts include digital leadership traits, organizational readiness, cross-functional collaboration, and customer-centricity.
  - Leadership is useful when the scenario shows internal disagreement or weak execution alignment.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Leadership readiness checklist.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Do not turn Chapter 1 into a generic leadership paragraph.
- Connection to midterm: One possible supporting framework area.

## Slide 7: Chapter 3 Support — Disruption and Adoption

- Purpose: Show when disruption/adoption logic fits.
- Key teaching points:
  - Use disruption when competitors, substitutes, adoption shifts, or changing customer expectations drive the problem.
  - Five Catalysts of Disruption help explain why the market is changing.
  - Diffusion-disruption logic helps explain whether adoption is accelerating, stalling, or moving into a different customer segment.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Disruption catalyst wheel linked to adoption movement.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Remind students that disruption is not just "new technology."
- Connection to midterm: One possible supporting framework area.

## Slide 8: Chapter 4 Support — Infrastructure and TOE/Stack Readiness

- Purpose: Show when infrastructure matters.
- Key teaching points:
  - Use TOE or Integrated Tech Stack when the recommendation depends on systems, data, app experience, integration, reliability, or readiness.
  - TOE connects technology, organization, and environment.
  - Stack logic helps students reason about digital utilities, source of truth, connective tissue, intelligence, and customer-facing experience.
  - Students should avoid proprietary system claims.
- Suggested visual or diagram: TOE triangle beside five-layer stack.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Keep infrastructure at the business capability level.
- Connection to midterm: One possible supporting framework area.

## Slide 9: Chapter 5 Support — Business Model Evolution

- Purpose: Show when business model logic is central.
- Key teaching points:
  - Use business model frameworks when the core issue involves revenue, value proposition, platform strategy, partners, customer relationship, or monetization.
  - Business Model Evolution Framework helps explain movement from product transaction to subscription, marketplace, or ecosystem logic.
  - Digital Business Model Canvas can highlight two or three changed elements.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Product-to-platform business model path.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Remind students not to fill every canvas box in a timed essay.
- Connection to midterm: One possible supporting framework area.

## Slide 10: Optional Chapter 6 Bridge — Insight/Data Capability

- Purpose: Clarify the optional bridge if the instructor permits it.
- Key teaching points:
  - Insight Value Chain may help explain what data or learning capability the recommendation requires.
  - Use it briefly if the recommendation depends on better customer insight, retention signals, engagement data, or experimentation.
  - Do not let Chapter 6 replace the required Chapter 1-5 synthesis unless the instructor says otherwise.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Capture, integrate, analyze, synthesize, plan, execute, measure loop.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Flag this as instructor-dependent.
- Connection to midterm: Optional bridge only.

## Slide 11: Evidence Discipline — Observed, Inferred, Recommended

- Purpose: Teach clear evidence boundaries.
- Key teaching points:
  - Observed means facts in the scenario or assigned course material.
  - Inferred means reasonable conclusions drawn from those facts.
  - Recommended means the student's proposed action.
  - Students should not invent current market data or company facts.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Three-column evidence table.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Connect evidence discipline to credibility.
- Connection to midterm: Supports scenario evidence and rubric alignment.

## Slide 12: Building a Recommendation, Metric, and Conclusion

- Purpose: Help students finish the essay with a decision.
- Key teaching points:
  - Strong answers recommend a path, not just analyze options.
  - The recommendation should address the central diagnosis.
  - Two or three implementation priorities should show sequence and tradeoffs.
  - One metric or success signal should match the recommendation.
  - The conclusion should restate the logic and decision clearly.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Recommendation stack: diagnosis, path, priorities, metric, conclusion.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Remind students to write a recommendation, not just analysis.
- Connection to midterm: Supports the final sections of the essay.
