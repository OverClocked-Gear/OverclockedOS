# Module 9 Instructional PowerPoint Outline: Content Strategy — Building Trust, Equity, and Engagement

Purpose: Create an instructor-facing outline for an explanatory PowerPoint/video that introduces Module 9 concepts, supports the content strategy assignment, and helps students understand how content connects to trust, brand equity, business outcomes, and measurement.

Explicit note: The video should teach content surfaces as examples of where strategy becomes visible. The Module 9 discussion requires students to inspect public content assets, but it does not require login, subscription, signup, or private account access.

## Slide 1: Module 9 Objectives

- Purpose: Introduce the learning goals for content strategy.
- Key teaching points:
  - Design content strategies aligned with business objectives.
  - Evaluate content effectiveness beyond engagement metrics.
  - Apply storytelling principles to compelling brand narratives.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Three-part objective map: strategy, measurement, storytelling.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Emphasize that content strategy connects creative work to business logic.
- Connection to assignment: Frames why students compare Red Bull and Mailchimp.

## Slide 2: Content Strategy Is Not Just Posting Content

- Purpose: Correct a common misconception.
- Key teaching points:
  - Content strategy is not a posting calendar.
  - Content should serve an audience need.
  - Content should support a business objective.
  - More content is not automatically better content.
- Suggested visual or diagram: "Posting" versus "content system" comparison.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Keep the distinction practical for undergraduate students.
- Connection to assignment: Prevents students from creating a content calendar instead of diagnosing the content system.

## Slide 3: Attention Is Scarce; Trust Is the Strategic Asset

- Purpose: Explain why content must do more than attract views.
- Key teaching points:
  - Attention is easy to count but hard to sustain.
  - Trust shapes whether audiences return, believe, share, buy, or recommend.
  - Brand equity grows when content consistently delivers value.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Attention -> Trust -> Equity -> Outcome chain.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Stress that engagement can be useful but is not the final goal.
- Connection to assignment: Supports the required comparison and metric selection.

## Slide 4: Content → Trust → Value Model

- Purpose: Introduce the required framework.
- Key teaching points:
  - Content provides value to an audience.
  - Valuable content can build trust.
  - Trust can support engagement, conversion, loyalty, advocacy, or brand equity.
  - Students should apply the model, not just name it.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Content -> Trust -> Engagement -> Conversion -> Loyalty -> Advocacy.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Use the framework as a diagnostic path.
- Connection to assignment: Required framework for both brands.

## Slide 5: Content as Audience Value

- Purpose: Help students identify what the content is doing for the audience.
- Key teaching points:
  - Content may educate, entertain, inspire, support, prove, or build identity.
  - The value depends on the audience and the decision context.
  - Different kinds of value create different kinds of trust.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Value-type wheel with examples.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Ask students to avoid generic claims like "it is engaging."
- Connection to assignment: Supports audience need and content value sections.

## Slide 6: Red Bull as a Content/Media Ecosystem Example

- Purpose: Position Red Bull's B2C content logic.
- Key teaching points:
  - Red Bull can be understood as a media-house/content ecosystem example.
  - The content often builds attention, culture, identity, and affinity beyond the product.
  - Students should analyze one public asset or hub, not the entire company.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Brand ecosystem map: event/story/video/community/identity.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Avoid unsupported current claims about performance or internal strategy.
- Connection to assignment: One required brand context.

## Slide 7: Mailchimp as Education and Trust-Building

- Purpose: Position Mailchimp's B2B/SMB content logic.
- Key teaching points:
  - Mailchimp Presents can be treated as an education, utility, storytelling, and trust-building example.
  - B2B/SMB content often reduces uncertainty, builds confidence, and supports decision-making.
  - Students should analyze one public asset or hub, not every content program.
- Suggested visual or diagram: SMB decision support map: problem -> learning -> confidence -> action.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Do not claim to know Mailchimp's internal content strategy or performance.
- Connection to assignment: One required brand context.

## Slide 8: B2C vs. B2B Content Differences

- Purpose: Help students compare the brands without flattening them.
- Key teaching points:
  - B2C content may lean more toward entertainment, culture, identity, or affinity.
  - B2B/SMB content may lean more toward education, proof, utility, and confidence.
  - Both can build trust and business value.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Side-by-side comparison table.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Avoid saying B2C is only fun or B2B is only rational.
- Connection to assignment: Supports the stronger-content-system comparison.

## Slide 9: Content Touchpoints

- Purpose: Show where content strategy becomes visible.
- Key teaching points:
  - Content touchpoints include hubs, stories, video, events, newsletters, guides, communities, podcasts, and tools.
  - A touchpoint should be analyzed for its job, not only its format.
  - Students inspect one public content asset or hub per brand.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Content touchpoint gallery or map.
- Optional instructor narration notes: No login, signup, subscription, or private access should be required.
- Connection to assignment: Supports asset selection.

## Slide 10: Moving Beyond Vanity Metrics

- Purpose: Teach measurement discipline.
- Key teaching points:
  - Views, likes, and followers may be incomplete indicators.
  - A stronger metric should connect to trust, decision support, loyalty, lead quality, conversion, or brand equity.
  - Metrics can be proposed even if the brand does not publish them.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Vanity metric versus business-linked metric ladder.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Students should explain why the metric fits.
- Connection to assignment: Supports the required business-linked metric.

## Slide 11: Business-Linked Content Metrics

- Purpose: Give students practical metric examples.
- Key teaching points:
  - Possible metrics include repeat visits, content-assisted conversion, newsletter signup intent, lead quality, time on useful content, return visits, branded search lift, customer-story progression, or loyalty engagement.
  - The right metric depends on the content's job.
  - Students should not claim private analytics are currently available.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Content job -> metric match table.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Keep examples as possible metrics, not claims about Red Bull or Mailchimp.
- Connection to assignment: Helps students select one matched metric.

## Slide 12: Using AI Responsibly for Content Strategy Brainstorming

- Purpose: Clarify AI's role.
- Key teaching points:
  - AI can help brainstorm audience needs, content value, and possible metrics.
  - AI output is not evidence of current company strategy, performance, audience data, or internal operations.
  - Students must verify public observations independently and disclose AI use.
- Suggested visual or diagram: AI as assistant, not evidence.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Reinforce course AI transparency language.
- Connection to assignment: Supports source and AI disclosure.

## Slide 13: How to Complete the Discussion Prompt

- Purpose: Walk students through the task.
- Key teaching points:
  - Choose one public Red Bull asset or hub.
  - Choose one public Mailchimp asset or hub.
  - Apply Content → Trust → Value to both.
  - Compare, recommend, and measure.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Four-step workflow.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Keep the response concise and analytical.
- Connection to assignment: Direct walkthrough.

## Slide 14: Assignment Checklist

- Purpose: Provide a final completion check.
- Key teaching points:
  - One asset or hub per brand.
  - Audience need identified.
  - Content value explained.
  - Trust or brand equity connection made.
  - Business outcome and metric included.
  - Sources, dates, and AI disclosure included.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Checklist.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Remind students not to create a full content calendar.
- Connection to assignment: Submission support.
