# Module 4 Instructional PowerPoint Outline: Capital One — Infrastructure, Trust, and Banking’s Next Horizon

Purpose: Create an instructor-facing PowerPoint/video outline that prepares undergraduate business students to reason about infrastructure without requiring engineering expertise. The outline supports the revised Module 4 discussion and shows how LLM-supported sensemaking can help students connect a public banking surface to TOE, Integrated Tech Stack, and Digital Horizon Planning.

## Slide 1: Infrastructure Is a Business Capability

- Purpose: Reframe infrastructure for business students.
- Key teaching points:
  - Infrastructure is not just servers, code, or technical plumbing.
  - Infrastructure shapes trust, reliability, identity continuity, service quality, speed, compliance, and customer confidence.
  - Business students can evaluate infrastructure by asking what must work behind the scenes for a public digital surface to keep its promise.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Customer-facing surface connected to behind-the-scenes capability blocks.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Emphasize that students are not being asked to identify Capital One’s internal architecture.
- Connection to assignment: Explains why a public Capital One surface can be used to infer infrastructure categories, not proprietary systems.

## Slide 2: Technical Architecture vs. Business Infrastructure Capability

- Purpose: Separate engineering detail from managerial reasoning.
- Key teaching points:
  - Technical architecture asks how systems are built.
  - Business infrastructure capability asks what the organization can reliably, securely, and responsibly do.
  - Students should focus on capability categories such as identity, data quality, integration, compliance, support, measurement, and reliability.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Two-column contrast: architecture detail vs. business capability.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Give examples of claims students should avoid, such as naming vendors or databases without evidence.
- Connection to assignment: Sets the boundary between observed evidence and reasonable inference.

## Slide 3: Capital One as an Infrastructure Learning Context

- Purpose: Introduce the banking context without unsupported current claims.
- Key teaching points:
  - Banking digital experiences carry high trust expectations.
  - Customers need confidence around identity, privacy, security, disclosures, continuity, and service recovery.
  - Regulated financial services must balance convenience with risk management.
  - The assignment does not ask students to claim current Capital One internal capabilities.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Trust equation: confidence + clarity + continuity + reliability.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Keep references broad and course-based; avoid current product claims unless instructor-verified.
- Connection to assignment: Anchors the customer job or trust issue students must identify.

## Slide 4: Tokenization in Plain Language

- Purpose: Explain future scenario language without turning the assignment into crypto investing.
- Key teaching points:
  - Tokenization means representing ownership, value, access, or rights as a digital token in a governed system.
  - Stablecoins and tokenized deposits are examples students may hear in financial-services conversations.
  - The course question is not whether these are good investments.
  - The course question is what infrastructure, identity, trust, compliance, settlement, and customer-experience capabilities would be needed if such capabilities became strategically relevant.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Asset or account claim represented as a digital token with governance and identity layers around it.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Repeat that no current Capital One crypto or tokenization capability should be claimed.
- Connection to assignment: Frames crypto/tokenization terms as scenario drivers only.

## Slide 5: Tokenization as Trust, Ownership, Settlement, Identity, Compliance, and Programmability

- Purpose: Show the operating issues behind the buzzwords.
- Key teaching points:
  - Trust: How does the customer know what is real, safe, and reversible?
  - Ownership: Who has the recognized claim?
  - Settlement: When is a transaction final?
  - Identity: Who is authorized to act?
  - Compliance: What rules, disclosures, monitoring, and escalation apply?
  - Programmability: What rules or automation can be embedded into the transaction?
- Suggested visual or diagram: Six-part operating capability map.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Use this slide to move students away from hype and toward infrastructure readiness.
- Connection to assignment: Helps students identify Horizon 3 capabilities to monitor but not rush.

## Slide 6: TOE Framework

- Purpose: Introduce the first required framework.
- Key teaching points:
  - Technology: capability, compatibility, risk, reliability, data, integration.
  - Organization: resources, skills, governance, process ownership, risk appetite.
  - Environment: regulation, customer expectations, competition, market conditions.
  - Strong infrastructure decisions sit at the intersection of all three.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Three-circle TOE Venn diagram.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Ask students to apply TOE to one surface issue, not the whole company.
- Connection to assignment: Students must use TOE to explain the banking infrastructure decision context.

## Slide 7: Integrated Tech Stack — Five Layers

- Purpose: Introduce the second required framework.
- Key teaching points:
  - Digital Utilities: foundational compute, security, and resilience.
  - Source of Truth: systems that maintain reliable records.
  - Connective Tissue: APIs, integrations, and data flows.
  - The Brain: analytics, intelligence, and decision support.
  - The Handshake: customer-facing websites, apps, portals, and interfaces.
  - Students should connect two or three relevant layers to the public surface.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Five-layer stack from Digital Utilities at the bottom to The Handshake at the top.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Explain that the public surface is usually The Handshake, but it depends on lower layers.
- Connection to assignment: Students must use the stack to explain reasonable infrastructure dependencies.

## Slide 8: Digital Horizon Planning / Three Horizons

- Purpose: Introduce strategic sequencing.
- Key teaching points:
  - Horizon 1: optimize today’s digital banking experience.
  - Horizon 2: extend platform readiness.
  - Horizon 3: future capability to monitor but not rush.
  - Good strategy separates what to improve now from what to prepare for next and what to watch carefully.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Three-horizon timeline from present optimization to future capability.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Emphasize that Horizon 3 is not automatically the recommendation.
- Connection to assignment: Students must provide a three-horizon analysis and recommend a Horizon 1 or Horizon 2 improvement.

## Slide 9: Using an LLM Responsibly as a Translator

- Purpose: Teach the required LLM support step.
- Key teaching points:
  - Use the LLM to explain unfamiliar technology in plain language.
  - Use it to generate hypotheses about possible infrastructure dependencies.
  - Use it to review the draft for unsupported claims or overreach.
  - Do not use AI output as evidence of current Capital One features, capabilities, or performance.
- Suggested visual or diagram: LLM support loop: translate, hypothesize, critique, verify, rewrite.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Model one prompt that tells the LLM not to make current company claims.
- Connection to assignment: Students must disclose tool, prompt summary, what they learned, and what they checked or rewrote.

## Slide 10: Avoiding Overclaiming

- Purpose: Make evidence discipline explicit.
- Key teaching points:
  - Observed: directly visible on the public surface or in assigned course material.
  - Inferred: reasonable infrastructure, data, identity, process, compliance, measurement, or operating dependency.
  - Recommended: the team’s proposed operating improvement.
  - Students must not claim to know proprietary systems, vendors, security controls, analytics, AI capabilities, blockchain capabilities, or crypto/tokenization offerings.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Three evidence labels with examples of acceptable language.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Contrast “Capital One uses X system” with “This surface would likely depend on a reliable identity or data layer.”
- Connection to assignment: Matches the required response labels.

## Slide 11: Assignment Walkthrough

- Purpose: Show students how to complete the discussion efficiently.
- Key teaching points:
  - Choose or use the assigned public surface.
  - Identify one customer job or trust issue.
  - Label observations and inferences.
  - Apply TOE, stack layers, and Three Horizons.
  - Recommend one Horizon 1 or Horizon 2 operating improvement.
  - Propose one matched metric.
  - Add sources and AI disclosure.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Checklist matching the required response structure.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Remind students the final response is approximately 300-400 words.
- Connection to assignment: Directly supports the student submission format.

## Slide 12: Metrics, Sources, and Final Checks

- Purpose: Help students finish with discipline.
- Key teaching points:
  - A metric should match the problem and recommendation.
  - Possible metric families include task completion, application-entry progression before personal data, support contact rate, customer confidence signal, onboarding completion, error rate, or trust-related feedback.
  - Metrics may be proposed; they do not need to be public.
  - Sources should include assigned course concepts and any public surface or source used.
  - AI disclosure should state tool, prompt summary, what it helped with, and what was verified, corrected, rejected, or rewritten.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Final submission quality check with metric, source, and AI disclosure boxes.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Close by reinforcing that the goal is responsible infrastructure sensemaking, not technical certainty.
- Connection to assignment: Supports rubric criteria for evidence and writing clarity.
