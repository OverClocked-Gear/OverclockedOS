# Module 3 Instructional PowerPoint Outline: Digital Disruption and Reinvention — Catalysts, Adoption, and Response

Purpose: Create an instructor-facing outline for an explanatory PowerPoint/video that introduces Module 3 concepts, supports the assignment, and helps students understand the connection between digital disruption frameworks and real digital operating touchpoints.

Note: The video should teach surfaces as examples of where disruption becomes visible, but Module 3 does not require students to inspect a live Tesla or competitor website/app.

## Slide 1: Digital Disruption Is a Pattern, Not a Surprise

- Purpose: Introduce the module's central idea.
- Key teaching points:
  - Disruption is not random.
  - Digital disruption usually follows recognizable patterns involving technology, customer behavior, economics, and operating models.
  - The goal is to diagnose why disruption gains momentum.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Funnel moving from weak signal to market shift to competitor response.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Emphasize that students are learning how to anticipate disruption, not simply describe famous disruptors.
- Connection to assignment: Frames Tesla as a case for diagnosing the disruption mechanism.

## Slide 2: What Disruption Is Not

- Purpose: Correct common misunderstandings.
- Key teaching points:
  - Disruption is not just "new technology."
  - A company can have advanced technology without changing adoption, customer expectations, or market economics.
  - A disruptive move usually changes what customers expect or what competitors must do to keep up.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Two-column contrast: new feature vs. disruptive shift.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Use student-friendly examples such as subscription, direct service, connected products, or self-service experiences.
- Connection to assignment: Helps students avoid writing only that Tesla was disruptive because it made EVs.

## Slide 3: Five Catalysts of Disruption

- Purpose: Introduce the required framework.
- Key teaching points:
  - Information Asymmetry: customers or firms gain better information.
  - Cost: the economics of buying, producing, distributing, or operating change.
  - Convenience: friction drops for customers or operators.
  - Capability: new functions become possible.
  - Connectivity: products, customers, firms, or systems become linked in new ways.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Five-spoke catalyst wheel with a center label, "Disruption Pressure."
- Optional instructor narration notes: Clarify that strong answers identify which catalysts matter most rather than listing all five.
- Connection to assignment: Students must use this framework to explain Tesla's disruption.

## Slide 4: Diffusion of Innovation

- Purpose: Explain adoption-stage logic.
- Key teaching points:
  - Innovations spread through different adopter groups.
  - Innovators and early adopters tolerate more uncertainty.
  - Mainstream buyers usually need proof, reliability, trust, and lower friction.
  - Adoption can accelerate, stall, or shift as customer expectations change.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Adoption curve labeled innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Keep the focus on customer adoption behavior, not total company size.
- Connection to assignment: Supports the adoption portion of the Tesla analysis.

## Slide 5: Crossing the Chasm

- Purpose: Explain the gap between early enthusiasm and mainstream adoption.
- Key teaching points:
  - The chasm is the difficult transition from early adopters to early majority.
  - Mainstream customers often want reliability, ecosystem support, clear value, lower risk, and social proof.
  - A disruptive company may generate attention before it wins broad adoption.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Adoption curve with a highlighted gap between early adopters and early majority.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Ask students to consider what would make a mainstream buyer hesitate.
- Connection to assignment: Students may use Crossing the Chasm to explain acceleration or friction in EV adoption.

## Slide 6: Diffusion–Disruption Grid

- Purpose: Connect adoption behavior to disruption catalysts.
- Key teaching points:
  - Disruption depends on both the strength of catalysts and the pattern of adoption.
  - Some innovations spread slowly despite strong technology.
  - Others accelerate when cost, convenience, capability, or connectivity align with customer readiness.
  - The grid helps explain why adoption may accelerate, stall, or skip expected stages.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Two-axis grid with adoption momentum on one axis and disruption pressure on the other.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Encourage students to explain movement, not just location.
- Connection to assignment: Optional framework for connecting Tesla catalysts to adoption-stage movement.

## Slide 7: Offensive vs. Defensive Posture

- Purpose: Prepare students to evaluate competitor response.
- Key teaching points:
  - Defensive posture protects the legacy model, margins, channels, or assumptions.
  - Offensive posture builds a new position, changes the operating model, or willingly challenges existing revenue logic.
  - Many firms show mixed posture: public innovation language with defensive operating choices.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Continuum from defend to adapt to attack.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Push students to evaluate the posture, not merely describe the competitor.
- Connection to assignment: Students must classify one competitor response as primarily defensive or offensive.

## Slide 8: Tesla as a Disruption Example

- Purpose: Apply the concepts to the focal company without requiring current facts.
- Key teaching points:
  - Tesla can be analyzed through product, software, direct customer relationship, connected vehicle, charging/support ecosystem, and ownership experience.
  - The strategic question is which catalysts made the disruption meaningful.
  - Students should avoid unsupported claims about current sales, app features, software, charging, or market share.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Map linking catalysts to possible Tesla-related operating implications.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Keep examples general and source-aware; do not present unsupported current data.
- Connection to assignment: Models the kind of catalyst reasoning students should use.

## Slide 9: Competitor Response: Defend, Adapt, or Attack

- Purpose: Show how to evaluate an incumbent or competitor.
- Key teaching points:
  - A competitor may defend its existing product line, distribution structure, service model, revenue logic, or customer relationship.
  - A competitor may adapt by adding EV products or digital features without fully changing its operating assumptions.
  - A competitor may attack by building a different customer experience, business model, or digital operating system.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Decision tree: defend legacy model, adapt current model, build disruptive response.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Remind students that competitor claims require source/date if they use current public information.
- Connection to assignment: Supports the required competitor posture evaluation.

## Slide 10: Where Disruption Becomes Visible

- Purpose: Connect strategic disruption to digital touchpoints and operating evidence.
- Key teaching points:
  - Disruption often becomes visible in public customer or operating experiences.
  - Possible examples include:
    - product research/configuration
    - app or connected-product experience
    - charging/support ecosystem
    - direct sales or service model
    - ownership lifecycle
    - software/update cadence
  - Students do not need to inspect a live website or app for this assignment.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Customer journey strip from research to purchase/configuration to ownership/support to update/retention.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Explain that a touchpoint is evidence of where strategy shows up, not a separate UX assignment.
- Connection to assignment: Supports the required touchpoint or operating implication.

## Slide 11: How to Use the Framework in the Discussion

- Purpose: Give students a practical response method.
- Key teaching points:
  - Start with the disruption shift.
  - Identify the most important catalyst or catalysts.
  - Connect the catalysts to adoption/chasm logic.
  - Evaluate one competitor response.
  - Add one touchpoint or operating implication.
  - Use evidence to support the judgment.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Response flowchart matching the suggested discussion structure.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Emphasize that framework application means using the model to make a judgment.
- Connection to assignment: Directly mirrors the student response structure.

## Slide 12: Evidence and AI/Source Transparency

- Purpose: Clarify source discipline and AI expectations.
- Key teaching points:
  - Observed means sourced facts, assigned course facts, or public data.
  - Inferred means reasonable adoption, competitor, or operating implications.
  - Judged / Recommended means the team's strategic evaluation.
  - Current claims need source and date.
  - AI output is not evidence of current company performance, product capability, market data, or competitor posture.
- Suggested visual or diagram: Three stacked evidence labels: Observed, Inferred, Judged / Recommended.
- Optional instructor narration notes: Remind students to disclose AI tool, purpose, and what they checked, corrected, rejected, or rewrote.
- Connection to assignment: Supports the evidence/source and AI transparency requirements.
