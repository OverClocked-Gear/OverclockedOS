# Web Strategy Discussion and Midterm Sequence Audit

## 1. Executive Summary

The discussion and midterm sequence is fundamentally sound. It follows a credible arc from leadership and strategy into disruption, infrastructure, business models, insights, consumer behavior, B2B platforms, content, media, cases, measurement, innovation, AI, and final synthesis. The strongest existing pattern is that most prompts ask students to compare recognizable companies, apply course concepts, and make strategic judgments rather than summarize readings.

The biggest alignment opportunity is framework transparency. Several prompts name frameworks that are not canonical in the Overclocked appendix, use older or external names without explaining their relationship to Overclocked, or require too many frameworks for a short team discussion. Each graded prompt needs a short framework contract that identifies required frameworks, optional extension frameworks, and frameworks not to force.

The biggest scaffolding opportunity is evidence discipline. Current prompts often ask for public data, examples, AI output, or current company observations, but they do not consistently require students to distinguish observed evidence, reasonable inference, and recommendations. This creates overclaim risk, especially in infrastructure, B2B platforms, AI, media, and public-company performance examples.

The biggest workload risks appear in Modules 5, 7, 9, 10, 11, and the Glossier case currently listed under Module 12 in the prompt inventory. These prompts combine multiple brands, multiple frameworks, AI prompting, exhibits/tables, outside evidence, and broad strategic synthesis. They may be manageable for case studies but are heavy for weekly undergraduate asynchronous discussions.

The course already works well where prompts ask students to make a comparative diagnosis and defend a strategic choice: Module 1, Module 2, Module 3, Module 5, the midterm, and the Nike case all have useful strategic tension. The sequence needs more transparency about how prompts connect to the four canonical rubric criteria, when AI is a tool rather than evidence, how many sources are expected, and whether peer replies are required.

## 3. Syllabus and Assessment Context

The course is an undergraduate online/asynchronous Web Strategy course delivered through ICON modules. Students progress as a cohort and complete weekly coursework online. The syllabus positions the first half of the course as framework development and the second half as applied digital business and marketing strategy.

The syllabus grading table lists 9 discussion submissions, including the intro discussion, for 420 points. The Course Work section separately says Discussion Assignments (11), and the course calendar includes Module 0 intro plus discussion submissions in Modules 1-5 and 7-9. Module 6 is the midterm week. Modules 10-14 are case, measurement, innovation, AI, and final-prep weeks rather than a clean continuation of weekly discussions.

The midterm is one online quiz/essay covering Modules 1-6, worth 100 points, with a 60-minute ICON time limit. The prompt inventory frames it as an integrative connected-fitness scenario requiring diagnosis, adaptability evaluation, and a strategic roadmap using Chapters 1-6.

Team submission expectations are clear at the syllabus level: discussion submissions are randomly assigned teams of two; case study groups are instructor-selected; students who do not contribute fairly may need to submit independently. The syllabus also says the group should write the vast majority of discussion and case submissions.

AI/source disclosure expectations are present in the syllabus and should be repeated in assignment prompts. Students may use AI tools, but must cite AI-generated material that informed the work, explain how AI informed the process and final product, validate AI-generated citations, and provide assignment-specific transparency such as final prompts when required.

Known syllabus inconsistencies to flag, not resolve:

- 9 discussion submissions versus 11 discussion assignments.
- 15-week language versus Week 1-16 calendar.
- Textbook naming inconsistency: "Digital Marketing Strategy: An Integrated Approach to Online Marketing" and "Overclocked: Business Reinvention."
- HBR case count lists Nike and Glossier, while the grading table requires three group cases and the calendar also includes Domino's.
- "1 Online Exams" wording for the final exam line.
- Module 0-15 numbering versus semester-week count.
- Module 12 has no separate due date in the syllabus calendar.
- Some case due dates are stated from both the prior and next week, which may be accurate operationally but needs Canvas clarity.

## 4. Sequence-Level Findings

The sequence progresses from foundational to applied thinking. Modules 1-3 establish leadership, strategy, and disruption; Modules 4-6 move into infrastructure, business models, and insights; Modules 7-9 apply consumer, B2B, and content concepts; later case prompts require broader synthesis.

The sequence does not fully avoid repeated assignment patterns. Many early prompts use "compare two companies, apply multiple frameworks, cite examples, include an image" as the default pattern. This is familiar for students, but it can flatten differences between leadership, strategy, disruption, infrastructure, and business model objectives.

The balance between conceptual diagnosis and practical application is uneven. Some prompts ask for strong strategic diagnosis but do not identify an operating decision, digital surface, customer job, or metric. Module 1 is closest to the newer design model because it adds a Digital Strategy / General Manager lens, public data points, a touchpoint connection, and AI/source disclosure.

Framework transparency is the largest sequence-level gap. Some prompts ask students to use named frameworks, but the required framework list is not always canonical, bounded, or aligned to the assigned chapter. Other prompts ask for several frameworks but do not explain which one governs the diagnosis.

Evidence and public data expectations are introduced early but not scaffolded consistently. Module 1 requires one data point per company with source and date. Later prompts ask for citations, real-world assets, AI output, public features, or external research, but do not always state what counts as evidence or how current observations should be verified.

The sequence generally supports asynchronous undergraduate workload when prompts are focused. Workload risk rises when prompts require multiple companies, multiple frameworks, AI prompt iteration, tables/exhibits, images, and broad outside research in the same weekly team discussion.

Team-based discussion structure is useful, but the prompt inventory does not consistently state whether peer replies are required, whether paired-team replies replace normal replies, or how team submissions map to Canvas discussion expectations.

The sequence prepares students for case studies and the midterm by building cumulative framework memory. The midterm covers Chapters 1-6 coherently. Case prompts ask for multi-framework integration, but they need clearer prioritization and rubric alignment to avoid becoming long lists of questions.

The sequence aligns with Overclocked's mastery-learning logic when it asks students to reuse prior chapters. The risk is framework overload: cumulative application should mean purposeful integration, not requiring students to mention every earlier concept.

## 11. Recommended Revision Roadmap

Phase 1: Light edits for framework transparency, evidence expectations, AI/source disclosure, and rubric clarity.

- Module 1: already close to target architecture; revise first as the pilot.
- Module 2: clarify Kinetic Strategy Framework or bounded strategy choice; remove old rubric weights.
- Module 3: clarify Five Catalysts as primary framework; source-date requirement for competitor posture.

Phase 2: Moderate revision with a light role/touchpoint cue.

- Module 5: narrow Peloton/Deere comparison to Business Model Evolution Framework, one operating dependency, and one metric.
- Module 7: recenter on digital consumer behavior rather than AI/media output as the main task.
- Module 8: clean copied instructions, set B2B platform framework contract, and define public evidence boundaries.
- Module 9: reduce output burden or clarify that tables are evidence for content-system diagnosis.

Phase 3: Full Brand Pods.

- Module 4: best candidate because infrastructure becomes more teachable when two teams inspect the same surface through Platform/Technology and Product/Experience lenses.
- Module 10: only if replacing existing case-adjacent discussion work; otherwise keep role lenses case-aligned and light.

Phase 4: Midterm prompt refinement.

- Midterm: preserve the connected-fitness scenario, but add a bounded response architecture: core diagnosis, framework-backed analysis, roadmap, and metric. Clarify that students should prioritize a small number of Chapter 1-6 frameworks rather than listing all concepts.

Phase 5: Case-study prompt alignment.

- Module 10 Domino's: prioritize opportunity selection and evidence boundaries.
- Module 11 Nike: compress into an executive recommendation architecture with a primary synthesis framework.
- Module 12 / Glossier case: confirm placement, framework names, AI transparency, and professional tone.

## 12. Module 1 Example Rewrite Strategy

Do not fully rewrite Module 1 yet. Recommended strategy:

- Preserve the original Microsoft vs. Nike leadership comparison because it gives students a clear incumbent transformation contrast.
- Clarify required frameworks by naming Five Traits of Digital Leadership as the required Overclocked framework and treating Gartner's Seven Traits as an assigned external lens if it remains required.
- Add a light Digital Strategy / General Manager lens that asks students to evaluate leadership alignment across vision, culture, systems, and customer outcomes.
- Add one leadership-to-digital-touchpoint cue, such as cloud services, app ecosystem, direct-to-consumer channels, owned media, personalization, data use, product roadmap, or customer relationship management.
- Clarify evidence/data expectations by requiring one sourced data point per company with source date, plus a distinction between observed company evidence and reasonable operating inference.
- Add AI/source disclosure language requiring tool, purpose, and what the team checked or rewrote.
- Avoid full Brand Pod structure because the module's purpose is foundational leadership comparison, not paired front-stage/back-stage operating analysis.

## 14. Overall Recommendation

The current prompt sequence is fundamentally sound. It should be revised, not replaced. The existing assignments already contain useful company contrasts, strategic tensions, and cumulative framework applications. Codex should preserve the underlying prompts and make an alignment pass that adds framework contracts, evidence discipline, AI/source disclosure, canonical rubric language, and manageable role/touchpoint cues.

Module 1 should be revised first as the next pilot because it is already closest to the desired model and can establish the repeatable assignment architecture for later light edits.

This audit supports a course-wide assignment alignment pass. The pass should proceed from light edits in Modules 1-3, then moderate revisions in Modules 5, 7, 8, and 9, then a deeper Brand Pod decision for Module 4, then midterm and case-study alignment.
