# Module 9 Discussion: Content Strategy — From Content to Trust to Business Value

## Draft Status

This is an instructor-facing working draft and a moderate revision of the existing Module 9 discussion. It preserves the Red Bull / Mailchimp content strategy comparison but reduces deliverable burden and clarifies the framework, evidence, metric, and AI/source expectations. It is not a Brand Pod and not a new assignment.

## Course Placement

- Course: Web Strategy
- Module: Module 9 — Content Strategy
- Audience: undergraduate
- Delivery: asynchronous
- Assessment type: existing team discussion
- Team structure: assigned partner/team of two, per syllabus

## Original Assignment Intent

The original prompt asks students to:

- compare Red Bull and Mailchimp content strategies
- classify content assets or archetypes
- diagnose how content creates engagement, trust, and/or business value
- reflect on AI-supported content strategy
- support analysis with evidence

This revision narrows the assignment because the original version asks for archetype classification, two full flywheel tables, AI prompt iteration, system diagnosis, and a 750-1,000 word response. The revised version reduces output burden, shifts from content classification to content-system diagnosis, makes the required framework explicit, requires one business-linked metric, and clarifies that AI output is process support, not evidence.

## Module Objective Alignment

Syllabus objectives:

- Design comprehensive content strategies aligned with business objectives.
- Develop frameworks for evaluating content effectiveness beyond engagement metrics.
- Apply storytelling principles to create compelling brand narratives.

The revised prompt supports these objectives by asking students to evaluate content as a system that connects audience need, storytelling or utility, trust creation, brand equity, and business outcomes. It moves students beyond judging whether content is entertaining or engaging and asks them to connect content to a strategic purpose and matched metric.

## Scenario: Content as a Trust and Value System

Red Bull and Mailchimp both use content to create value beyond product promotion, but they do so in different contexts. Red Bull behaves like a media brand that uses entertainment, events, culture, and identity to build attention and affinity. Mailchimp Presents uses content to educate, support, and build trust with entrepreneurs, small businesses, and marketing decision-makers.

Students should evaluate how each brand's content system moves from:

- audience need
- to content value
- to trust
- to business outcome

Clarifications:

- The assignment does not require a full content calendar.
- The assignment does not require a full media plan.
- Students should inspect one public content asset or content hub per brand.
- Students should not claim to know internal content strategy, performance metrics, audience data, or CRM segmentation.

## Framework Contract

Required:

- Content → Trust → Value Model

Students should use the model to explain:

- what value the content provides
- how that value builds trust
- how trust supports a business outcome

Optional extensions:

- Content Strategy Pyramid, if confirmed as canonical
- Content Archetype Matrix, if confirmed as canonical
- Three-Layer Content Flywheel, if confirmed as canonical
- Agile Content Capability Matrix, if confirmed as canonical
- Trust Amplification Model, if confirmed as canonical

Do not force:

- full customer journey map
- full content calendar
- AI-generated content strategy as the main deliverable

Students must apply Content → Trust → Value to one content asset or hub from each brand. They should identify what audience need the content serves, how the content creates trust or brand equity, one business outcome the content could support, and one metric that goes beyond surface engagement.

## Role and Touchpoint Cue

Use a light role lens within one team submission.

Primary role lenses:

- Digital Merchandising & Content
- Analytics & Experimentation

Supporting lens:

- Digital Strategy / General Manager

From the Digital Merchandising & Content perspective, students should ask:

- What audience need does this content serve?
- What story, utility, or experience is the content delivering?
- How does the content fit the brand's positioning and customer relationship?
- How does the content reduce uncertainty, educate, entertain, inspire, or build affinity?

From the Analytics & Experimentation perspective, students should ask:

- What outcome should this content influence?
- What metric would indicate whether the content is creating business value?
- How would the team know whether the content is building trust, not merely generating clicks?

- Which brand has the stronger content system, and why?

## Student-Facing Prompt Draft

Compare Red Bull and Mailchimp as examples of content strategy.

1. Select one public content asset or content hub from Red Bull and one from Mailchimp.
2. Use the Content → Trust → Value Model to analyze each brand.
3. Identify the audience need each content asset addresses.
4. Explain what kind of value the content provides: education, entertainment, inspiration, utility, identity, community, proof, or confidence.
5. Explain how the content could build trust or brand equity.
6. Identify one business outcome each brand's content could support.
7. Compare which brand has the stronger content system and why.
8. Recommend one improvement for one of the two brands.
9. Propose one business-linked metric and explain why it fits.
10. Use at least one cited source or assigned course example. If using a current public content asset, include source/date.
11. Include AI/source transparency.

## Evidence Discipline

Require students to distinguish:

- Observed: content asset, message, format, audience cue, call to action, source/date, or assigned course fact.
- Inferred: reasonable conclusion about audience need, trust-building, brand equity, or business purpose.
- Recommended: the team's proposed content improvement or metric.

Students should not invent current Red Bull or Mailchimp content performance, audience data, strategy, metrics, personalization logic, creator partnerships, or internal decision-making.

AI output may help brainstorm content value, audience needs, or possible metrics, but it is not evidence of current brand strategy or performance.

## Strong-Answer Indicators

- Selects one specific public content asset or hub per brand.
- Applies Content → Trust → Value rather than merely naming it.
- Identifies a real audience need.
- Distinguishes engagement from trust or business value.
- Explains why the content fits the brand's narrative or audience.
- Connects content to one business outcome.
- Compares Red Bull and Mailchimp rather than summarizing them separately.
- Recommends a specific improvement.
- Proposes a metric beyond vanity engagement.
- Cites public content assets with source/date.
- Discloses AI/source use transparently.

## Weak-Answer Indicators

- Describes content without strategic diagnosis.
- Focuses only on likes, views, or followers.
- Treats content strategy as posting more often.
- Lists content formats without explaining audience need.
- Claims to know internal performance or strategy.
- Treats AI output as evidence.
- Recommends generic "make more videos" or "post on TikTok" without rationale.
- Fails to connect content to trust, business outcome, or metric.
- Creates a full content calendar instead of answering the prompt.

## Common Student Misconceptions

- Content strategy is the same as social posting.
- Engagement automatically means business value.
- Storytelling is separate from strategy.
- B2B content has to be boring.
- B2C content is only entertainment.
- Trust can be manufactured quickly.
- AI-generated ideas are evidence.
- The best content strategy is always more content.
- A metric must already be publicly available.
