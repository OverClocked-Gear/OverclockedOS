# Module 3 Discussion: Digital Disruption and Reinvention — Tesla, Adoption, and Competitive Response

## Draft Status

This is an instructor-facing working draft and a light revision of the existing Module 3 discussion. It preserves the original Tesla, adoption/chasm, and competitor-response focus while making the framework contract, evidence expectations, role lens, AI/source transparency, and canonical rubric alignment more explicit.

This is not a Brand Pod and not a new assignment.

## Course Placement

- Course: Web Strategy
- Module: Module 3 — Digital Disruption and Reinvention
- Audience: undergraduate
- Delivery: asynchronous
- Assessment type: existing team discussion
- Team structure: assigned partner/team of two, per syllabus

## Original Assignment Intent

The original Module 3 prompt asks students to:

- analyze Tesla through adoption and chasm logic
- evaluate how digital disruption unfolds in an industry
- assess a defensive competitor response
- apply disruption catalysts from Chapter 3
- support the analysis with evidence

The revised version preserves that intent but clarifies which framework is required, which adoption frameworks may support the answer, and how students should distinguish observed facts from strategic interpretation.

## Module Objective Alignment

Module 3 objectives in the syllabus are:

- Create a framework for evaluating and anticipating digital disruption
- Evaluate how organizations respond to digital disruption, from defensive to offensive postures
- Apply the technology adoption curve to digital innovation diffusion

The revised prompt supports these objectives by asking students to use the Five Catalysts of Disruption to explain why Tesla's disruption gained momentum, then connect those catalysts to adoption/chasm logic and one competitor response. The assignment also asks students to translate disruption into customer-facing or operating implications, helping them see how disruption mechanisms affect adoption, competitive posture, and digital operating strategy.

## Framework Contract

### Required Framework

- Five Catalysts of Disruption

Students must use the Five Catalysts of Disruption to explain what made Tesla's disruption accelerate, stall, or become more defensible. The canonical framework appendix identifies five catalysts: Information Asymmetry, Cost, Convenience, Capability, and Connectivity.

### Optional Extensions

- Diffusion of Innovation
- Crossing the Chasm
- Diffusion–Disruption Grid
- Offensive vs. Defensive Posture
- Kinetic Strategy Framework, only if used briefly to explain competitor adaptability or velocity

### Do Not Force

- full infrastructure stack analysis
- full business model analysis
- media/content frameworks
- AI/future-state frameworks
- a full Kinetic Strategy quadrant analysis unless the instructor explicitly asks for it

### Framework Use Expectations

Students should:

- identify which catalyst or catalysts mattered most
- connect those catalysts to adoption-stage movement or chasm crossing
- evaluate whether a competitor response is defensive or offensive
- use the framework to make a strategic judgment, not merely define terms

## Role and Touchpoint Cue

Use a light role lens only:

- Digital Strategy / General Manager
- Digital Product & Experience as a supporting cue

Students should think like leaders deciding whether to defend the legacy model, adapt the current offering, or build a more disruptive response.

Students may identify one place where disruption becomes visible in the customer or operating experience, such as:

- EV product research page
- mobile app or connected-vehicle experience
- charging/support ecosystem
- direct sales or configuration experience
- over-the-air software update logic
- ownership/service experience
- competitor digital evidence or public feature comparison

Do not require live website/app inspection.

## Student-Facing Prompt Draft

Tesla is often discussed as a disruptive company, but disruption is not just about having new technology. For this discussion, analyze Tesla as an example of digital disruption and compare one incumbent or competitor response.

In your team response:

1. Briefly explain the disruptive shift Tesla represented in the automotive market.
2. Apply the Five Catalysts of Disruption to explain which catalyst or catalysts most accelerated Tesla's adoption.
3. Use Diffusion of Innovation, Crossing the Chasm, or the Diffusion–Disruption Grid to explain where adoption accelerated, stalled, or crossed into a broader market.
4. Choose one incumbent or competitor and evaluate whether its response was primarily defensive or offensive.
5. Identify one customer-facing or operating touchpoint where the disruption became visible.
6. Use at least one cited source or assigned reading example. If you use current public data or competitor claims, include the source and date.
7. Include AI/source transparency.

Your goal is not to praise or criticize Tesla in general. Your goal is to explain the disruption mechanism, evaluate adoption logic, and make a strategic judgment about how a competitor responded.

## Evidence Discipline

Students should distinguish:

- Observed: sourced company facts, assigned case/course facts, or public data
- Inferred: reasonable adoption, competitor, or operating implications
- Judged / Recommended: the team's evaluation of disruption posture and competitor response

Students should not invent current data. Any public or current claims about Tesla, competitors, EV sales, charging, software, pricing, adoption, market share, apps, or product capabilities should include source and date.

If the original assignment's image requirement is retained, the image should directly support the disruption/adoption analysis and be briefly explained as evidence. Otherwise, the image requirement should be removed to keep the assignment focused on analysis rather than illustration.

## Suggested Response Structure

1. Tesla's disruptive shift
2. Catalyst analysis
3. Adoption/chasm analysis
4. Competitor response: defensive or offensive
5. Digital touchpoint or operating implication
6. Evidence/source
7. AI/source disclosure

## Canonical Rubric Alignment

Use only the four canonical criteria.

| Assignment requirement | Applicable rubric criterion | Evidence expected | Common grading concern |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Explain Tesla's disruptive shift | Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning | Clear statement of what changed in the market, customer experience, operating model, or competitive logic | Treating disruption as a generic label or simply saying Tesla used new technology |
| Apply the Five Catalysts of Disruption | Application of Course Frameworks with Specific Examples | Specific catalyst or catalysts connected to Tesla's adoption momentum or friction | Listing all catalysts without explaining which mattered most |
| Use adoption/chasm logic | Application of Course Frameworks with Specific Examples | Explanation of adoption stage, chasm risk, acceleration, stall, or broader-market movement | Confusing adoption stages with company growth or brand awareness |
| Evaluate one competitor response | Strategic Diagnosis and Critical Reasoning | Judgment that the response is defensive, offensive, adaptive, or mixed, supported by evidence | Describing a competitor without evaluating posture |
| Identify one touchpoint or operating implication | Use of Examples and Evidence | Specific customer-facing or operating example connected to disruption | Adding a surface example that is not connected to the framework analysis |
| Use cited evidence | Use of Examples and Evidence | Assigned reading example or cited public source; source/date for current claims | Unsupported claims about current EV sales, charging, software, apps, or market share |
| Organize response and cite course concepts | Writing Clarity, Organization, and Proper Citation of Course Concepts | Clear structure, accurate framework names, course concept citation, AI/source disclosure | Overly broad response, missing citations, or AI output presented as evidence |

## Strong-Answer Indicators

- identifies the disruption mechanism rather than simply praising Tesla
- applies the Five Catalysts of Disruption to explain adoption momentum or friction
- distinguishes diffusion/adoption stages from competitive response
- explains what it means to cross the chasm or stall before mainstream adoption
- evaluates one competitor response as defensive or offensive with evidence
- connects disruption to a customer-facing or operating touchpoint
- uses at least one relevant cited source or assigned reading example
- makes a clear strategic judgment
- discloses AI/source use transparently

## Weak-Answer Indicators

- says Tesla is disruptive without explaining the catalyst
- treats disruption as only "new technology"
- confuses innovation adoption with company growth
- describes a competitor without evaluating posture
- uses unsupported claims about current EV sales, market share, charging, software, or app features
- name-drops Diffusion of Innovation or Crossing the Chasm without applying it
- uses an image without explaining its analytical relevance
- uses AI output as evidence without verification

## Workload Estimate

Estimated team workload: about 2 to 3 hours total for an asynchronous two-person discussion. This includes reviewing the assigned materials, selecting one competitor, identifying one evidence source or assigned reading example, drafting the response, coordinating with the teammate, and revising for clarity.

This is a normal Module 3 discussion workload, not an added activity.

## AI and Source Transparency

Students should disclose:

- which AI tool, if any, they used
- the purpose of use, such as brainstorming, outlining, editing, or checking clarity
- what the team checked, corrected, rejected, or rewrote

AI output is not evidence of current company performance, current product capability, current market data, or current competitor posture. Students remain responsible for verifying current claims with cited sources and for writing the majority of the final submission.
